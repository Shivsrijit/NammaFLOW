
1. Predictive Hotspot Heatmap – Identify and forecast illegal parking hotspots across the city with a time-based heatmap.

The map must feel instant: filter changes should reflect in well under a second, which means you precompute clusters and scores offline and serve cached results rather than clustering live on every request. The demo must be bulletproof, so cache everything and have a fallback static dataset loaded locally; nothing kills a hackathon pitch like a live API timing out on stage. The impact score and forecasts must be interpretable, every number explainable in one sentence. The priority queue should be usable by a non-technical traffic officer, which argues for a clean, mobile-friendly layout since these people are in the field. And the whole thing should degrade gracefully: if the forecast model isn't ready, the historical baseline still fills that view.

  Feature 5: Congestion Impact Index (CII) — The Heart of "Quantify Impact"

  Problem it solves: Your problem statement explicitly asks to "quantify their impact on traffic flow." Your current
  priority score is a weighted composite. CII makes that number translate directly into lane-equivalent congestion,
  which a traffic officer or judge can reason about instantly.

  Why it's unique: Every other team will rank zones by violation count. You'll rank them by carriageway blockage
  percentage, which is a fundamentally different and more honest unit of traffic impact.

  Technical implementation:

  CII = vehicle_footprint × road_lane_count × violation_density × time_persistence × proximity_factor

  Where:
  - vehicle_footprint = estimated lane-area blocked per vehicle type
    (SCOOTER=0.4 lane-m, CAR=0.8, MAXI-CAB=1.2, TANKER=2.5 lane-m)
  - road_lane_count = lanes on the road this hotspot sits on (OSM data)
  - violation_density = violations per geohash cell per hour
  - time_persistence = how many hours across the dataset this cell was active
  - proximity_factor = 1.0 if at intersection, 1.5 if blocking a metro exit, 2.0 if near hospital/emergency route

  - Road lane count comes from OSM Overpass API (highway=primary → 2 lanes typically, highway=trunk → 3, etc.)
  - Metro station POIs from OSM too
  - CII outputs a number between 0–100 that you label as: Low / Medium / High / Critical with clear thresholds
  - The Critical threshold is the key insight: a TANKER on a 2-lane road at a metro exit junction scores 25+; a SCOOTER
  on a side road scores <3. This is defensible, not arbitrary.

  Difficulty: Medium. OSM query + feature engineering + one formula.

  Pitch line for judges: "We're not ranking zones by how many violations happened. We're ranking them by how much road
  they choked."

  Feature 6: Temporal Violation Fingerprint (Time-of-Day + Congestion Correlation)

  Problem it solves: Your problem statement says enforcement is "patrol-based and reactive." Knowing when a zone
  becomes dangerous matters as much as knowing where. If a commercial-area hotspot only lights up at 6 PM on Fridays,
  sending a patrol there at 9 AM is useless.

  Why it's unique: Most teams will show a static heatmap. A temporal fingerprint per hotspot shows the violation
  lifecycle, letting the patrol engine recommend not just where but when.

  Technical implementation:

  - For each hotspot cluster, build a 24×7 matrix: rows = hours (0–23), columns = days of week (Mon–Sun), cell value =
  average violation count in that hour-slot across the dataset
  - This gives each hotspot a fingerprint: peak hours, off-peak quiet windows, weekday vs weekend patterns
  - Cross-reference with known congestion patterns: if your fingerprint shows peak violations at 6–8 PM near a
  commercial area, that's rush-hour compounding — flag it as dual-threat (parking violation + rush-hour congestion)
  - Surface this as a mini heatmap inside each hotspot's detail panel
  - The patrol recommendation engine (Feature 3) consumes this directly: "Zone 7 is hot on Fridays 5–8 PM — schedule a
  patrol unit for Friday 4:30 PM arrival"

  Difficulty: Easy. Pure pandas groupby on created_date with hour extraction.

  Resume angle: Time-series pattern recognition, operational analytics.

  Pitch line: "Our system doesn't just know where to send an officer. It knows when."

  Feature 7: Dark-Zone Detector (Unenforced High-Risk Zones)

  Problem it solves: Your dataset's created_by, device_id, and center_code columns are not just metadata — they're
  evidence of enforcement bias. A hotspot near a police station may have high violations because patrols happened to
  drive there, not because it's genuinely worse. Conversely, a zone with zero violations in your data may be a genuine
  black spot where nobody ever went to check.

  Why it's unique: This is the single most differentiating analytical insight in your entire project. Almost no team
  will think of it. It directly addresses your own problem statement's admission that "enforcement is patrol-based and
  reactive."

  Technical implementation:

  Two-part analysis:

  Part A — Coverage gap detection:
  - Count unique device_id and created_by entries per geohash6 cell
  - Cells with high violation density but low officer diversity → over-patrolled (officers keep returning to the same
  visible hotspot)
  - Cells with zero violations but high OSM POI density (metro, commercial, hospital) and no device_id coverage → dark
  zone (likely violations happening, never enforced)
  - These dark zones get a special badge: "⚠ No Enforcement History — High Congestion Risk"

  Part B — False-negative estimation:
  - Use OSM POI density and road type as features, train a simple regressor on cells that DO have enforcement:
  violation_count ~ POI_density × road_capacity × proximity_to_transport_hub
  - Extrapolate to dark zones: "Based on POI density and road profile, this zone should average 12 violations/week.
  Zero recorded = estimated false-negative rate of 95%."
  - This number goes into your priority queue as an estimated undetected violations column

  Difficulty: Medium. Part A is aggregation queries. Part B is one sklearn.linear_model call.

  Pitch line: "The zones with zero violations aren't clean — they're unvisited. Here's what we'd find if we looked."

  Feature 8: Spillover Congestion Propagation Model (optional)

  Problem it solves: The problem statement says spillover parking "choke carriageways and intersections." A violation
  at point A doesn't just affect point A — it backs up the adjacent intersection, which cascades to the next signal,
  and so on. Your current hotspot map shows dots. The propagation model shows ripple effects.

  Why it's unique: This turns your hotspot map from a "violation atlas" into a "congestion chain" view. Judges who are
  engineers or city administrators will immediately see the operational value.

  Technical implementation:

  - Pull Bengaluru road network from OSM (Overpass API, one query)
  - Build a directed graph: nodes = intersections, edges = road segments
  - For each hotspot, identify the road segment it occupies (nearest OSM way to the violation centroid)
  - Apply a capacity degradation model:
  if vehicle_type == TANKER and lanes == 2:
      effective_lanes = 1   # one lane blocked
      downstream_capacity_reduction = 0.4  # 40% of normal
  if vehicle_type == CAR and lanes == 2:
      effective_lanes = 1.5
      downstream_capacity_reduction = 0.2
  - Run BFS (not Dijkstra — we're not optimizing routes, just spreading impact) from the hotspot node outward, reducing
  capacity by downstream_capacity_reduction × 0.7^hops per level
  - Stop when reduction < 5% (the cascade fades)
  - Color-code the propagation on your map: hotspot = red, first ring = orange, second ring = yellow, faded = gray
  - The CII (Feature 5) feeds directly into this: higher CII → wider propagation radius

  Difficulty: Medium-Hard. OSM graph construction is ~2 hours of work. BFS propagation per hotspot is a loop,
  precomputable.

  Demo moment: Click any hotspot → cascade ripples outward → adjacent roads light up → officer sees "this one violation
  could affect this entire corridor."

  Feature 9: Enforcement Efficiency Scorecard

  Problem it solves: The problem statement says it's "difficult to prioritize enforcement zones." The efficiency
  scorecard answers: given limited resources, where does one hour of patrol time produce the maximum reduction in
  congestion impact?

  Why it's unique: Priority ranking by raw CII tells you the worst zones. The efficiency scorecard tells you which
  zones give you the best return on your enforcement hour — critical when you have 5 officers and 20 hotspots.

  Technical implementation:

  Efficiency Score = CII_of_zone / estimated_enforcement_cost_to_reach_zone

  Where:
  - CII_of_zone = from Feature 5
  - estimated_enforcement_cost =
      distance_from_nearest_station × traffic_delay_factor × officer_count_available_at_that_station

  - distance_from_nearest_station = haversine from hotspot centroid to police station centroid (precompute per zone)
  - traffic_delay_factor = estimated travel time from OSM road distance / average speed on those roads
  - Officers per station = count of unique created_by entries for that police_station

  Output: a spend-vs-return table — zone, CII, cost, efficiency score, and recommended action ("High Efficiency: Deploy
  here first").

  - This also handles the "not enough officers" problem narratively: "You have 47 hotspots rated High. You have
  resources for 12. Here's the 12 that give you the most impact per patrol-hour."

  Difficulty: Easy. All aggregation queries + one arithmetic formula per hotspot.

  Pitch line: "You can't be everywhere. Our system tells you where one hour of your time is worth the most."
