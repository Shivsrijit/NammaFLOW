What this dataset really is
Despite the framing around "parking-induced congestion," what you have is not parking data and not traffic data. It's enforcement data, essentially Bengaluru Traffic Police e-challan / violation records from around November 2023. Every row is a single recorded violation event: a CAR or SCOOTER or TANKER caught at a lat-long, tagged with a violation type (WRONG PARKING, NO PARKING), an offence code, the police station and sometimes a junction, who recorded it, on which device, and whether it was later validated (approved/rejected). That reframing matters a lot, so hold onto it.
What it's genuinely strong for
The spatial and temporal columns are clean enough to build real hotspot intelligence. You have latitude, longitude, location name, police_station, junction_name, plus created_date and modified_date with timestamps. That combination lets you do exactly the kind of work you already did in the Flipkart competition. You can bin violations into geohash cells (geohash6 or even finer geohash7 for street-level resolution), run density estimation or DBSCAN/HDBSCAN clustering on the raw coordinates, and produce a spatiotemporal heatmap of where and when illegal parking concentrates. Time-of-day and day-of-week slicing will almost certainly reveal sharp patterns near the commercial and metro zones the problem mentions. That heatmap alone directly answers the "no heatmap exists today" pain point.
The hard part, and where teams will win or lose
The problem asks you to "quantify their impact on traffic flow." Read your columns again: there is no speed, no congestion, no volume, no flow anywhere in this data. You cannot measure impact on traffic flow from a violation table, full stop. So you have three honest paths, and naming this gap explicitly in your pitch is itself a strong move:
You can build a proxy for impact using only internal signals, for example weighting a violation by vehicle type (a TANKER or MAXI-CAB blocking a lane is a heavier impact than a SCOOTER), by offence severity, by whether it sits on a named junction versus "No Junction," and by clustering density. That gives you a defensible "congestion impact score" without external data.
You can enrich with external data, which is what I'd push for if time allows. OpenStreetMap gives you the road network, road widths, and POIs like metro stations and commercial centers for free. A live or historical traffic-speed source (TomTom, HERE, or Google's APIs) would let you actually correlate violation density against measured slowdowns at the same coordinates and time. Even one afternoon of pulling OSM road context turns your hotspots from "dots" into "this violation is on a two-lane arterial 80 metres from a metro exit."
Or you treat impact qualitatively and lean on the spatial story, which is the weakest of the three but still viable for a hackathon if your visualization is sharp.
A trap worth flagging, because it's subtle
Enforcement data is generated by enforcement. A cell with many violations might be a genuine parking nightmare, or it might just be where patrols happen to go, which the problem statement itself admits is "patrol-based and reactive." So a naive violation heatmap risks measuring police behavior rather than parking behavior. You can defend against this using device_id, created_by, and center_code to estimate enforcement coverage, then normalize violation counts by patrol presence. If your team raises this confound and corrects for it, you'll sound like the only people in the room who actually understood the data. It's the same selection-bias instinct you'd apply to a leaderboard that overfits.
Data quality notes from the screenshots
A few things jumped out that you'll want to handle in cleaning. Several columns are almost entirely NULL (description, closed_date, action_taken, and one of the two data_sent columns), so they're probably droppable. The validation field carries approved/rejected, which is quietly valuable: it's effectively a label for whether a flagged violation was real, so you can filter to approved-only for a clean hotspot map, and separately you could train a small false-positive classifier on it if you want an extra ML angle. There's also both a vehicle_type and an updated_vehicle_type (CAR corrected to MAXI-CAB, and so on), which is a human-correction signal worth a footnote. The vehicle numbers look masked/uniform (FKN00GL0...), so plate-level tracking of repeat offenders probably won't work. And I'd build a small legend for the offence codes rather than guessing: from the data itself, 113 lines up with NO PARKING and 112 with WRONG PARKING, while 104 and 107 appear as co-occurring codes, so confirm those against any data dictionary the organizers gave you before asserting meanings on stage.

ML models (in priority order, not all required)
Think of these as three tiers. Tier 1 is non-negotiable and is your safe, certain deliverable. Tier 2 is your "wow." Tier 3 is bonus only if days 1вЂ“3 go smoothly.
Tier 1 вЂ” Hotspot detection (unsupervised, build day 1вЂ“2). Run DBSCAN or HDBSCAN directly on the latitude/longitude of approved violations. HDBSCAN is better here because it finds clusters of varying density without you hand-tuning a radius, which matters when a metro-station hotspot is tight and a commercial-strip one is spread out. Each cluster becomes a "hotspot" object. Alongside this, compute a kernel density estimate (KDE) over a geohash6/geohash7 grid to render the smooth heatmap surface. This is the literal answer to "no heatmap exists today" and it cannot fail, which is why it's tier 1.
Tier 1 вЂ” Impact / Priority score (this is the heart, build day 2). This is what answers "quantify impact" and "hard to prioritize." Make it a transparent weighted composite, not a black box, because judges who aren't ML people need to follow it. The score for each hotspot combines: violation density, a vehicle-severity weight (a TANKER or MAXI-CAB blocking a lane hurts flow far more than a SCOOTER, so weight them up), persistence (does this cell light up repeatedly across days and hours, or was it a one-off?), road criticality and proximity to metro/commercial POIs from OpenStreetMap, and critically, a normalization by enforcement coverage. Keep every component visible in the UI so the score decomposes on click. Interpretability beats accuracy here.
Tier 2 вЂ” Predictive enforcement (your wow, build day 3, your Flipkart skillset). This is the proactive flip and it's exactly the spatiotemporal forecasting you already did on Kaggle. Train a LightGBM regressor to predict violation count per geohash cell per hour-of-week, using lag features, hour-of-day, day-of-week, POI density, road type, and rolling historical means. The output powers a "tomorrow 9 AM, these five zones will likely be hot, send patrols here" recommendation. This is the single line that wins it: reactive becomes predictive. T4 x2 isn't even needed, this trains in seconds, but the framing lands hard.
Tier 3 вЂ” Enforcement-bias correction model (the credibility layer). Use device_id, created_by, and center_code to estimate patrol coverage per area, then normalize raw counts by it so you're measuring parking behavior rather than police presence. You can fold this into the impact score (cleanest) rather than building a separate model. Mention it on stage even if it's simple.
Tier 3 вЂ” False-positive classifier (optional extra ML angle). The validation column (approved/rejected) is a free label. A small classifier predicting whether a flagged violation will be approved gives you a "confidence" signal and a second ML artifact if a judge asks "what else did you do." Nice to have, not a priority.
If you're behind on day 3, cut Tier 3 entirely and even cut the LightGBM down to a simple historical-average baseline that still produces a forecast. Never cut Tier 1.
Functional requirements (what the system does)
The product is a single interactive map dashboard with supporting views. Functionally it must: ingest and clean the violation data, filtering to approved records by default; detect and render parking hotspots as clusters on a live map; let the user filter by time-of-day, day-of-week, and vehicle type, with the map updating in response; display a heatmap density layer toggleable over the clusters; compute and show an impact/priority score per hotspot, with a breakdown of its components on click; produce a ranked enforcement queue (a sortable table of zones, worst first) that an officer could act on directly; forecast hot zones for the next time window and surface them as a "patrol recommendation"; and present an honest methodology view explaining the enforcement-bias correction. Optionally it lets a user simulate "if we enforce zone X, here's the estimated knock-on effect," which makes for a killer closing demo moment if you have time.
API + presentation endpoints
On the backend (I'd use FastAPI since it sits naturally next to your Python ML and serves JSON fast), expose roughly these:
GET /api/hotspots with query params for time, day, and vehicle type, returning clustered hotspots as GeoJSON with their scores. GET /api/heatmap returning the KDE density grid for the surface layer. GET /api/hotspot/{id} returning the detail for one zone: vehicle mix, temporal profile, and the decomposed score components. GET /api/forecast returning predicted hot zones for the next horizon. GET /api/priority-queue returning the ranked enforcement list. GET /api/stats returning the headline KPIs for the dashboard tiles. And optionally GET /api/patrol-route returning a suggested order of stops for a given police station, and POST /api/simulate for the what-if.
On the frontend, the views (presentation endpoints) are: the map dashboard as the hero landing view; a hotspot detail panel that slides in on click; a temporal analytics view showing the hour Г— day heatmap so the patterns are undeniable; the priority queue table; the forecast view with the patrol recommendation; and a short methodology / about page that exists purely to impress judges with your bias-correction honesty. For the stack, React plus Leaflet or deck.gl gives you a polished, fast map; if your team is small and frontend isn't a strength, Streamlit gets you 80% of the demo in 30% of the time, at the cost of looking less custom. For a hackathon I lean React + deck.gl if even one teammate is comfortable with it, Streamlit if not.
Non-functional requirements (how well it does it)
The map must feel instant: filter changes should reflect in well under a second, which means you precompute clusters and scores offline and serve cached results rather than clustering live on every request. The demo must be bulletproof, so cache everything and have a fallback static dataset loaded locally; nothing kills a hackathon pitch like a live API timing out on stage. The impact score and forecasts must be interpretable, every number explainable in one sentence. The priority queue should be usable by a non-technical traffic officer, which argues for a clean, mobile-friendly layout since these people are in the field. And the whole thing should degrade gracefully: if the forecast model isn't ready, the historical baseline still fills that view.

Why CV isn't required here
Look back at what you actually have. Your dataset is a table of already-recorded violations, challan records with lat-long, vehicle type, timestamp, and validation status. The detection has already happened, by a human officer, in November 2023. There are no images and no video feed anywhere in your data. So the question your problem statement asks is not "is this specific car illegally parked right now?" It's "given thousands of violations that were already detected, where are the hotspots, how bad is each one, and where should we send patrols next?" That's a spatiotemporal analytics and forecasting problem, which is exactly the tier-1-through-tier-2 work I laid out. Computer vision would be answering a question nobody asked you.
Why it's a trap even if it sounds impressive
Real-time illegal-parking CV is a genuinely hard problem that companies spend years on. You'd need a camera feed or dashcam footage you don't have, vehicle detection, then the much harder part of inferring legality, which requires knowing where no-parking zones are, reading signage, distinguishing a stopped car from a parked one, handling occlusion and night and rain. None of that is in your dataset, and building a toy version on borrowed footage would eat three of your five days to produce something that doesn't even connect to the data the organizers gave you. Judges notice when a flashy component is bolted on and disconnected from the actual problem. It reads as "we wanted to show off CV" rather than "we solved the problem."
